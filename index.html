The Road to Revolution & The American Revolution (1754-1800)

The central theme of Period 3 is the shift from British subjects to American citizens.

Phase 1: The Seeds of Conflict (1763-1765)

The end of the French and Indian War (Seven Years' War) is the turning point that changes everything.

Why it Matters: Britain wins, but is left with a massive war debt and a huge new empire in North America to manage.

Key Change: The era of Salutary Neglect (Britain leaving the colonies to govern themselves) is OVER. Britain now needs to tax the colonies to pay for the war and the new territory.

Key British Actions & Colonial Responses:

British Action (The "What") Year Colonial Response (The "So What")
Proclamation of 1763 1763 Bans settlement west of the Appalachians. Angers colonists who just fought a war for that land. Seen as British limitation on their freedom.
Sugar Act 1764 First act intended specifically to raise revenue. Cracks down on smuggling. Angers merchants.
Stamp Act 1765 THE BIG ONE. A direct tax on all paper goods. Affects almost every colonist, especially the elite.
Stamp Act Congress 1765 First significant intercolonial meeting. 9 colonies send delegates. They petition Parliament, arguing "No Taxation Without Representation."
Sons & Daughters of Liberty 1765 Secret societies formed to resist British policies. Use boycotts (most effective tool), intimidation, tar-and-feathering.
Virtual Representation 1765 Britain's argument: "You are represented in Parliament because its members represent all British subjects, not just the district they are from." Colonists reject this completely.

Phase 2: Escalation & Rising Tensions (1767-1774)

The conflict intensifies from a debate over taxes to a struggle over liberty and authority.

British Action (The "What") Year Colonial Response (The "So What")
Townshend Acts 1767 Taxes on imported goods (glass, tea, paper). Revenue used to pay royal officials, making them loyal to the Crown, not the colonies.
Letters from a Farmer in Pennsylvania (John Dickinson) 1767 Argues that Parliament has no right to tax the colonies for revenue. Reinforces the "No Taxation" argument using Enlightenment ideas.
Boston Massacre 1770 British soldiers fire on a mob, killing 5 colonists. Paul Revere's engraving is used as propaganda to galvanize anti-British sentiment.
Committees of Correspondence 1772+ Created by Samuel Adams. A communication network between colonies to share news and coordinate resistance. Builds intercolonial unity.
Tea Act 1773 Not a new tax, but a monopoly granted to the British East India Company. Hurts colonial merchants.
Boston Tea Party 1773 Sons of Liberty dump 342 chests of tea into Boston Harbor as a direct protest against the Tea Act.
Coercive (Intolerable) Acts 1774 Britain's punishment for the Tea Party: Closes Boston Harbor, restricts Massachusetts assembly, expands Quartering Act.
Quebec Act 1774 Angers colonists for different reasons: extends Quebec's territory into the Ohio Valley and allows Catholicism. Seen as an attack on their land and religion.
First Continental Congress 1774 12 colonies (not GA) meet. Goal is NOT independence. They create: 1) Declaration of Rights and Grievances, 2) The Association to enforce a boycott of British goods. Agree to meet again in 1775 if demands aren't met.

Phase 3: The Philosophical Shift (1775-1776)

This is the "Why" behind the "What." How did the colonists justify rebellion?

Enlightenment Influences:

  • John Locke (Natural Rights): People are born with Life, Liberty, and Property. Government's job is to protect these. If it fails, the people have a right to rebel. (Jefferson changes "Property" to "Pursuit of Happiness").
  • Jean-Jacques Rousseau (Social Contract): Government power comes from the consent of the governed. People agree to be governed in exchange for the protection of their rights.
  • Baron de Montesquieu (Separation of Powers): Power should be divided within government to prevent tyranny.

Thomas Paine's Common Sense (Jan. 1776):

  • The Game Changer. Written in plain language for the masses.
  • Argues for independence, calls monarchy "tyrannical," and says America should be a "republic."
  • Convinces thousands of common colonists that independence is not just possible, but necessary.

The Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776):

  • Authored by Thomas Jefferson.
  • Heavily influenced by Enlightenment ideas (Locke).
  • Structure:
    1. Preamble: States purpose and philosophy (natural rights, social contract).
    2. List of Grievances: Long list of complaints against King George III (focusing on the King was a strategic way to unite colonists).
    3. Resolution of Independence: Declares the colonies "free and independent states."

Phase 4: The War for Independence (1775-1783)

Key Fact: The fighting starts over a year before independence is declared.

Key Events:

  • Lexington & Concord (April 1775): "The Shot Heard 'Round the World." British troops sent to seize colonial arms and arrest leaders. Paul Revere's ride warns the militia. The war begins.
  • Second Continental Congress (1775):
    • Now acts as the first national government.
    • Creates the Continental Army and appoints George Washington as commander.
    • Issues the Olive Branch Petition (last-ditch effort for peace, which the King rejects).
    • Ultimately drafts and adopts the Declaration of Independence.

Key to American Victory: How did they win?

  1. Home-Field Advantage & Guerrilla Tactics: They knew the land and used unconventional tactics against the rigid British formations.
  2. Ideological Commitment: Patriots were fighting for their homes, liberty, and the creation of a new nation. This is a more powerful motivator than a British soldier fighting for pay.
  3. George Washington's Leadership: His ability to hold the army together through terrible hardships (like Valley Forge) was crucial. At Valley Forge, Baron von Steuben trained the army into a professional fighting force.
  4. Foreign Aid (The Decisive Factor):
    • Battle of Saratoga (1777): A major American victory. This is the turning point of the war because it convinces France that the Americans can win.
    • French Alliance: France provides money, troops, and most importantly, its navy. Spanish and Dutch support also follows.

Final Victory & Treaty:

  • Yorktown (1781): The final major battle. The French navy blocks a British escape by sea, while Washington's army (with French troops) surrounds them on land. British General Cornwallis surrenders.
  • Treaty of Paris (1783):
    • Britain officially recognizes American independence.
    • U.S. borders are set: Atlantic to Mississippi River, Great Lakes to Florida.
    • America must honor debts owed to British merchants.

Thematic Takeaways for Your Essay

Causation: The Revolution was caused by a gradual shift from political dispute (taxation) to an ideological struggle (liberty vs. tyranny), fueled by Enlightenment thought.

American & National Identity: The experience of resisting British policies (Stamp Act Congress, Committees of Correspondence, Continental Congresses) forged a new American identity separate from Britain.

The "Why Win?": Remember the four factors: Home-field advantage, ideological commitment, Washington's leadership, and the critical French alliance after Saratoga.

Timeline of Key Events Leading to the American Revolution

Date and Event Brief description of what happened Impact it would have
1754: French and Indian War Begins A war sparked by colonial rivalries over the Ohio River Valley. British American colonists and their Indian allies fought against the French and their Indian allies. It was the North American theater of the global Seven Years' War. This costly war, though a British victory, created massive war debt for Britain. To pay it off, Britain would impose new taxes on the colonies, directly leading to the conflict that followed.
Oct. 7, 1763: Proclamation of 1763 After winning the war, the British government issued a proclamation that forbade American colonists from settling west of the Appalachian Mountains, reserving that land for Native American tribes. This angered colonists and land speculators who wanted to expand westward. It was the first major post-war act that made colonists feel their interests were subordinate to British policy, breeding resentment.
Apr. 5, 1764: The Sugar Act Parliament imposed duties on foreign goods like wine, coffee, and sugar imported into the colonies. It also expanded the power of the customs service and navy to crack down on smugglers using harsh courts without juries. This was one of the first acts designed to raise revenue from the colonies, not just regulate trade. It set a precedent for taxation without colonial consent and angered merchants.
Mar. 22, 1765: The Stamp Act This law required colonists to pay a direct tax on almost every piece of printed paper they used, including newspapers, legal documents, and playing cards. The tax had to be paid in British currency. This was the first direct, internal tax levied on the colonists. It sparked widespread, violent protest, organized boycotts of British goods, and led to the slogan "No taxation without representation."
Aug. 14, 1765: Colonial Reaction to Stamp Act In response to the Stamp Act, colonists organized boycotts of British goods, held protests, and rioted. Tax collectors were attacked and forced to resign, making the act unenforceable. The unified and violent colonial reaction demonstrated their willingness to resist British policy. It showed the power of economic boycotts and forced Parliament to eventually repeal the act.
Oct. 11, 1768: British Troops Arrive in Boston In response to growing unrest and protests against the Townshend Acts, Britain sent two regiments of soldiers to Boston to restore order and protect royal officials. The presence of a standing army in peacetime was seen as a threat to liberty by the colonists. It heightened tensions dramatically and made violent confrontation more likely, culminating in the Boston Massacre.
July 28, 1769: Land Purchase from Six Nations As part of the policy established by the Proclamation of 1763, British authorities purchased land from the Six Nations Iroquois Confederacy to manage westward expansion and prevent conflict. This represented the British government's attempt to control the pace of settlement and maintain a stable frontier. However, it failed to satisfy land-hungry colonists or protect Native American lands in the long term.
1767-1768: Townshend Acts Imposed Chancellor Townshend imposed new duties on common imported goods like glass, lead, paint, paper, and tea. Most critically, the revenue was used to pay the salaries of royal governors and judges. This removed the power of colonial assemblies to control these officials by withholding their pay, making them loyal only to London. It further eroded colonial self-government and led to new waves of protest and non-importation agreements.

Answers to Questions from the Historical Context (Page 111-112)

What tighter control?

After the Seven Years' War, Britain ended its policy of "salutary neglect" and began asserting more direct control over the colonies. This included:

  • The Proclamation of 1763: Restricting colonial expansion west of the Appalachian Mountains.
  • New Taxes: The Sugar Act (1764) and Stamp Act (1765) were designed to raise revenue directly from the colonies, which was a new form of economic control.
  • Enforcement of Trade Laws: Strengthening vice-admiralty courts to crack down on smuggling and ensure compliance with trade regulations.

What ideas of "self government"? Where did these come from?

The ideas of self-government came from:

  • Local Traditions: For over 100 years, many colonies had been governing their internal affairs through their own elected assemblies (like the Virginia House of Burgesses) which controlled budgets and passed local laws.
  • British Precedent: The tradition of English liberties, such as the right to a trial by jury and the principle that one's property could not be taken without consent (through representation in Parliament).
  • Enlightenment Ideas: The writings of John Locke and others, which argued that governments derive their power from the consent of the governed.

What taxes?

  • The Sugar Act (1764): A tax on imported sugar and molasses.
  • The Stamp Act (1765): A direct tax on all printed materials (newspapers, legal documents, playing cards).
  • The Townshend Acts (1767): Taxes on imported goods like glass, lead, paint, paper, and tea.

What is meant by "colonial representation"?

The colonists believed in "actual representation," meaning they were only obligated to pay taxes passed by their own elected representatives in their colonial assemblies. The British government argued for "virtual representation," the idea that all members of Parliament represented the interests of all British subjects, including the colonists, even though the colonists could not vote for them. The colonists rejected this concept.

Why might colonists feel "constraints on economic activities"?

  • Taxes: New taxes like the Sugar Act and Townshend Acts increased the cost of doing business.
  • Suppression of Smuggling: British efforts to enforce trade laws and stop smuggling threatened the lucrative, if illegal, trade that many colonial merchants relied on.
  • Monopolies: Acts like the Tea Act (1773) granted a monopoly to the British East India Company, undercutting colonial merchants.

Why might colonists feel "constraints on political rights"?

  • Dissolving Assemblies: British governors dissolved colonial assemblies (like in Massachusetts after the Circular Letter) for protesting British policies, stripping colonists of their political voice.
  • Trial Without Jury: The expansion of vice-admiralty courts meant colonists could be tried for smuggling without a jury of their peers.
  • The Massachusetts Government Act: Part of the Intolerable Acts, it revoked Massachusetts' charter and took away its long-held right to self-government.

Calls for resistance based on the 4 ideas:

Idea Description & Historical Examples
Rights of British subjects Colonists argued they were entitled to the traditional "rights of Englishmen," such as no taxation without consent. Patrick Henry's Stamp Act Resolutions and the phrase "no taxation without representation" embody this.
The rights of the individual This was based on Enlightenment natural rights (life, liberty, property). James Otis argued that no government could rightfully deprive a man of these rights. The Declaration of Independence later enshrined this.
Local traditions of self-rule A history of governing through their own assemblies. The formation of the Stamp Act Congress and the First Continental Congress were examples of colonists using their tradition of assembling to govern their own affairs.
The ideas of the Enlightenment Republicanism (fear of concentrated power, virtue of the people) and John Locke's social contract (the right to overthrow a government that violates your rights). These ideas are the philosophical backbone of the Declaration of Independence.

History Brief: The Intolerable Acts

Question Answer
Why pass these ACTS? To punish Boston for the Boston Tea Party and to reassert British control over Massachusetts.
Identify 5 things colonists disliked
  1. Closing Boston Harbor.
  2. Ending self-government in Massachusetts.
  3. Allowing trials of officials to be moved to England.
  4. Quartering troops in private homes.
  5. Appointing a military general (Thomas Gage) as governor.
Attitude from Parliament They were angry and dismissive, believing the colonies were weak and would quickly submit. Lord North said, "We must control them or submit to them."

History Brief: The First Continental Congress

Question Answer
Purpose of the meeting? To decide how to respond to the Intolerable Acts.
What did everyone agree upon? To continue boycotting British goods and to prepare their militias for war.
What did people disagree on? How far to go; some wanted peace at all costs, while others (like Patrick Henry) believed war was inevitable.
Main outcome? The creation of the Continental Association to enforce the boycott and a decision to meet again in 1775 if grievances were not addressed.
Patrick Henry's attitude? He was a radical who believed war had already begun. His famous quote: "Give me liberty, or give me death!"
Who are the minutemen? Colonial militiamen who vowed to be ready to fight at a moment's notice.

Common Sense by Shmoop

Question Answer
Who was Thomas Paine? An English-born journalist and propagandist who immigrated to America with Benjamin Franklin's help.
What did he write? The pamphlet Common Sense (1776).
Main Ideas
  1. Independence from Britain.
  2. Republican democracy is the best government; monarchy is unnatural.
  3. A call to action for colonists to fight for their liberty.
Why was he successful? He used powerful rhetoric, wrote in straightforward language, and overturned common assumptions about monarchy, making the case for independence relatable to ordinary people.

Question Answer

6 Major Causes of the America Revolution

Cause What was it? How did it further split the colonies from Great Britain?
Proclamation of 1763 Banned colonial settlement west of the Appalachians. Colonists felt betrayed after fighting for that land and saw it as an effort to keep them weak and controlled.
Sugar Act (1764) A tax on molasses, strictly enforced. It angered merchants and established the precedent of Britain raising revenue from the colonies, leading to cries of "no taxation without representation."
Stamp Act (1765) A direct tax on all printed materials. It united the colonies in protest (Stamp Act Congress) and created organized resistance groups (Sons of Liberty).
Townshend Acts (1767) Taxes on imported goods. Led to new boycotts and protests, and the stationing of British troops in Boston, which increased tension and led to the Boston Massacre.
Tea Act (1773) Granted a monopoly on tea to the British East India Company. Seen as a trick to accept taxation, it provoked the Boston Tea Party, a direct act of defiance.
Intolerable Acts (1774) Punitive laws to punish Massachusetts for the Tea Party. They were seen as a threat to the liberty of all colonies, leading directly to the First Continental Congress and the unification of colonial resistance.

  5. (117) Briefly describe the purpose of Committees of Correspondence?

    Their purpose was to serve as the principal channel for sharing information, shaping public opinion, and building cooperation among the colonies.

  6. (118) What is the image all about? In your opinion, how does this make the colonists look?

    What it's about: The image depicts a Boston mob tarring and feathering a British customs official, John Malcolm, as punishment for his actions. The Boston Tea Party is shown in the background.

    How it makes the colonists look: (Opinion) It makes the colonists look violent, intimidating, and ruthless. While the text mentions it was embraced by Patriots, the details (hot tar, feathers, noose) portray a mob using brutal public humiliation and the threat of death to enforce their will.

  7. (120) Why did the British go to Lexington and Concord? What happened there?

    Why: General Gage sent troops to Concord to seize the colonial armory and stores of weapons and gunpowder.

    What happened: At Lexington, the British encountered a group of minutemen. Shots were fired, killing eight Americans. The British moved on to Concord, burned some supplies, and were then attacked by a growing militia force on their retreat back to Boston, suffering heavy casualties (73 killed, 202 wounded).

    Will You Rebel For A Cause So Trivial? (Boucher) The Sun Never Shone On A Cause of Greater Worth (T-Paine)

    2. Christians should submit to disappointments and calamities peacefully, following the example of Christ and his disciples, rather than resorting to violence. 2. The period of debate is over; the king has chosen arms, and the continent has accepted the challenge.
    3. The rebellion is based on the reports of others (agitators) and not on the colonists' own direct discovery of oppression. 3. The relationship with Britain is broken beyond repair, comparing it to a rape that cannot be forgiven. America must become an "asylum for mankind."

Key Terms

Term (Page #) What is important about the event or idea? Why do we remember it?
Yorktown (138) What: The last major battle of the American Revolution, where a combined American and French force trapped General Cornwallis's army, forcing its surrender. Importance: It was the decisive military victory that effectively ended the war and ensured American independence.
The Articles of Confederation (139) What: The first written constitution of the United States, which created a national government with limited powers and most authority retained by the states. Importance: It was the first attempt at a national government, but its weaknesses (e.g., no power to tax) led to the need for the current U.S. Constitution.
Northwest Ordinance of 1787 (145) What: A law that established a system for creating new states in the territory north of the Ohio River. Importance: It set a pattern for the orderly expansion of the United States, banned slavery in the Northwest Territory, and guaranteed rights like trial by jury and public education.
Bill of Rights (148) What: A list of essential rights and liberties, such as freedom of speech and religion, added to the U.S. Constitution as the first ten amendments. Importance: It was a key outcome of the "Revolution of the Mind," protecting individual freedoms from government infringement and mirroring similar declarations in early state constitutions.
